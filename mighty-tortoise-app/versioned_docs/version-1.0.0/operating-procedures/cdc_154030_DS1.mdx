## Introduction and Rationale

### Why Include Environmental Lead Sampling in Lead Prevalence Studies?

Environmental lead sampling serves several critical purposes:

- **Source Characterization**: Collection of air, water, soil and dust characterizes the sources of lead in the environment
- **Study Component**: It can be a component of a lead exposure prevalence study
- **Standards Comparison**: It can allow comparison to local and international standards

> ⚠️ **Important Note**: Many standards (e.g. the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency action level for water of 15 parts per billion [ppb]) are pragmatic, meaning they can be reached using good lead control techniques, rather than being health-based. A health-based standard would establish a 'safe' level of exposure and this has not been identified for lead.

> 💡 **Instructor Note**: Environmental sampling can be used to identify the major sources of lead in a child's environment and direct remediation efforts

---

## Environmental Lead Sources

Many sources of lead can contribute to blood lead levels:

| Source Category | Examples |
|----------------|----------|
| **Transportation** | Gasoline (no longer a major source in most countries) |
| **Industrial** | Battery recycling, Electronic waste, Mines/smelters |
| **Consumer Products** | Lead paint, Some traditional medicines |
| **Informal Industries** | Unregulated or cottage industries, Child labor operations |

## What Environmental Sampling Can Tell You

### Location and Mapping Benefits
- **Contamination Mapping**: Location of areas of lead contamination, enabling mapping of areas of high and low lead concentrations
- **Source Identification**: Helps identify source(s) of exposure in population known to have high blood lead levels
- **Risk Assessment**: Identifies at-risk populations who should have blood lead levels checked
- **Exposure Reduction**: Provides exposed populations with measures to reduce or stop exposure

### Remediation Support
- **Data-Driven Decisions**: Data can be used to direct and evaluate remediation efforts

## Initial Procedures and Planning

### Important Factors to Consider

#### Community Engagement
- Talk to community members/heads of households to find out about likely areas of environmental contamination and of exposure
- Use this information to guide the sampling strategy

#### Sampling Strategy
- **Random Selection**: Randomly select households/sampling sites
- **Sample Size**: Calculate the sample size needed and use a simple randomization strategy to select households/sites where environmental media will be collected

#### Documentation Requirements
- **GPS Coordinates**: Be sure to take a GPS reading at every participating household and wherever soil is sampled
- **Record Keeping**: Note the coordinates on the sampling form (provides data for mapping—helps to identify commonalities in geographic area of interest)

#### Sample Areas to Identify
- **Painted surfaces**: walls, window frames
- **Dust wipe areas**: near entrance to home, child's play area, child's sleeping area, window frames  
- **Water samples**: water storage vessel, tap, well
- **Soil sample**: bare soil where child plays

#### Label Management
- Be sure the person responsible for environmental sampling has the correct labels by household enrollment number
- Know where to place labels and which sample collection containers to use
- Write the sample identification (ID No.) and any notes on the sample collection form

## Sample Collection Methods

### Paint Analysis

#### Equipment and Method
- **In-Situ Testing**: Paint analysis can be done in-situ using portable x-ray fluorescence analyzer (XRF)
- **Quality Control**: Confirm XRF testing by sending duplicate samples (10%–15% of total) to a laboratory with the necessary proficiencies
- **Training Required**: Training in the use of XRF devices is essential
- **Reference**: For further details about paint analysis, see Module C.ii

### Water Sampling

#### Required Supplies
- Gloves
- Water ID label  
- 125-mL collection container
- Environmental sampling form

#### Procedure

> 💡 **Tip**: If collecting multiple sample types, collect the water sample first and use the same gloves for the dust and soil sampling. Levels of lead contamination in water are far lower than in dust/soil so cross-contamination is unlikely.

**Steps:**
1. **Source Selection**: Take the water sample from the source that the family is using for drinking/cooking
2. **Labeling**: Place label on sampling container
3. **Safety**: Put on gloves
4. **Collection**: Fill the sample container with water from source
5. **Sealing**: Seal the container

> 📋 **Instructor Note**: Collect a grab sample from the water source the family is using for cooking and drinking on the day of your visit. For the purposes of a prevalence study, a grab sample can provide some useful information about lead in water. Water sampling for regulatory purposes is much more complex and beyond the scope of a prevalence study.

### Dust Wipe Sampling

#### Required Supplies
- Gloves (use gloves from the water sampling effort)
- Dust ID labels
- Sampling wipes  
- Ruler or template (can be reused for each sampling frame)
- Sample container
- Baby wipe for dust sampling
- Rubbish bag

#### Getting Started
- **Glove Continuity**: Keep gloves on from the water sampling effort
- **Labeling**: Place 'DUST' label on sampling containers
- **Location Selection**: Identify floor location near the entrance and the floor area where the child plays. Identify window near to area where child sleeps
- **Preparation**: Clear large debris from the sampling area before beginning the sampling. **DO NOT clean the area before sampling**

#### Floor Wipe Procedure

**Surface Requirements:**
- If the entrance and play area locations are covered with fixed carpet/matting, choose an alternative area with a hard surface (e.g., wood floor, concrete, linoleum) and note the location on the sampling form
- If there is a door mat, remove the mat and sample under it

**Sampling Steps:**
1. **Measurement**: Measure a 10-cm x 10-cm (100 cm²) space with ruler for each floor location
2. **Sample Collection**: Remove wipe from packet and obtain the sample as indicated in the Wipe Schematic section below
3. **Container Management**: Use one wipe for the sample near the door and one wipe for the sample in the area where the child plays
4. **Storage**: Place each wipe into separate sample containers with separate labels
5. **Documentation**: Fill in the sampling form

### Soil Sampling

#### Required Supplies
- Gloves
- Soil ID label
- Sample scoop
- Large sealable bag (e.g., 4 liters/1 gallon)  
- Rubbish bag

#### Getting Started
- **Location Selection**: Identify the area outside where children play (ask the parent or guardian)
- **Surface Requirements**: Sample only from areas that are not covered by grass—the area should be bare soil
- **Preparation**: Place soil sample label on sample bag
- **Safety**: Put on gloves (or keep on gloves from water and dust sampling)

#### Sampling Procedure
1. **Sample Collection**: Take five discrete samples—take the sample from the top 1–1.5 cm (½ inch) of soil using the disposable scoop
2. **Mixing**: Put all five samples in the sample bag, filling the bag by at least a third, and manipulate it to mix the samples
3. **Cleanup**: Put the gloves in the rubbish bag  
4. **Documentation**: Fill in the sample form and note the GPS location of the soil sample

### Air Sample Collection

#### General Process Steps

**Particulate Sample Collection:**
1. **Filter Preparation**: Filter is equilibrated (conditioned) to a specified temperature and pressure and then weighed before being sent to the field for sampling [typically done by an environmental laboratory]
2. **Transport**: Filter transported to site
3. **Installation**: Filter placed in sampler inlet. Sample is run at specified date, time, and duration
4. **Recovery**: Filter recovered from sampler, total flow of sample recorded from sampler. Sample is transported to lab (or location to be weighed)

#### Additional Considerations
- Filter preparation and laboratory liaison
- Impactor preparation
- Power outlets
- Ambient moisture  
- Security of instrument
- Returning rented equipment

> ⚠️ **Instructor Note**: The manufacturer will provide detailed written instructions and/or hands-on training. It is important to consider these other issues when placing and using the equipment. If the power supply cannot be assured, battery operated models are available. However, transport of lead acid batteries may be complicated across international boundaries. Make sure you can get them back!

## Final Steps and Documentation

### Sample Management
- **Consolidation**: Place all environmental samples together in one bag when completed
- **GPS Documentation**: Take and record GPS points at each home and each soil sampling location. Record these points on the sampling form
- **Record Keeping**: Record sample IDs and any notes on the sampling form. Place a label on the sampling form as well
- **Quality Check**: Make sure all samples are appropriately labeled

## Advanced Techniques

### Use of Lead Isotope Ratios

#### Scientific Basis
- **Isotope Types**: Four main isotopes of lead are 208, 206, 207, 204
- **Source Variation**: Ratio of the isotopes varies by the source of the ore  
- **Environmental Mixing**: Isotope ratio of soils represents mixing of lead from various ores used in gasoline, consumer products, and smelting
- **Fingerprinting**: If isotope ratio in a lead source and in blood can be characterized, then this can be useful 'fingerprinting' of environmental pollution
